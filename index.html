Alberta Multifamily Finance Update – February 2026

A monthly look at transactions, financing conditions, and housing data across Alberta’s multifamily market.
Edition: February 2026 Prepared by Joshua Oswald
Focus: CMHC-insured multifamily & conventional term debt in Alberta (Edmonton, Calgary, and key secondary markets).

1. Market Snapshot

  • Sentiment: Investor appetite remains constructive, supported by Alberta’s relative affordability and continued demand for rental housing.
  • Rent fundamentals: CMHC reports Canada’s purpose-built vacancy rate rose to 3.1% in 2025 (from 2.2% in 2024). Edmonton’s purpose-built vacancy rate rose to 3.8%. Rent growth slowed in several major markets and incentives became more common in higher-end/newer product.
  • Transactions: Market activity is being driven by a mix of private capital, local groups scaling platforms, and selective institutional capital targeting best-in-class locations and durable cashflow.

Quick take: Conditions look more balanced than the ultra-tight 2022–2024 period. Underwriting is increasingly about realistic lease-up, expense normalization, and “new tenant” rent assumptions rather than straight-line growth.

Edmonton

    • Reported consideration: $37.0M (off-market) • Implied pricing reported at ~$210k/suite and ~4.7% cap in industry coverage

  • ICE District (Connect Centre) – retail podium + air rights

Why this matters for multifamily investors

  • “Best nodes win”: capital continues to pay up for locations with hard-to-replicate amenities and long-term demand drivers.
  • Air rights + mixed-use: downtown deals increasingly blend stabilized income with embedded future density optionality.

3. Interest Rates & Credit Conditions

Bank of Canada & Government of Canada Yields as of Feb 2026

  • BoC policy rate: 2.25% (held on Jan 28, 2026).
  • GoC “marketable bond average yields” (BoC table): as of Feb 2, 2026: 1–3y 2.51%, 3–5y 2.87%, 5–10y 3.22%, 10y+ 3.82%.

Implications for CMHC-Insured Multifamily

  • Pricing mechanics: insured coupons remain anchored to GoC yields plus lender/insurer spreads, with execution heavily influenced by leverage, DSCR, property condition, and sponsor profile.
  • Credit posture: lenders are still conservative on value-add underwriting, leaning on in-place cash flow and defensible turnover rent assumptions.

Underwriting lens: With vacancies up and incentives more common, be explicit in your model about (i) “new tenant” rent, (ii) lease-up velocity, and (iii) concessions.

4. Housing Starts & Rental Fundamentals

Housing Starts (Latest CMHC table)

  • December 2025 starts (Alberta): 3,082 total (of which 1,526 apartments).
  • December 2025 starts (Edmonton CMA): 1,714 total (of which 968 apartments).
  • December 2025 starts (Calgary CMA): 1,245 total (of which 535 apartments).

Rental Market (CMHC 2025 RMS)

  • Edmonton vacancy (purpose-built): 3.8% in 2025.
  • Edmonton turnover rent (2BR): $1,600 (Oct 2025), up from $1,560 in 2024.
  • National signal: vacancy rising and turnover rents flattening/declining in some major centres; incentives more frequent in newer/high-end supply.

Investor takeaway: Alberta supply is still coming, and CMHC data suggests softer conditions versus the tightest years. For acquisitions, “in-place vs. pro-forma” needs a tougher haircut than 2022–2024.

5. Policy & CMHC Notes (What actually changes the model)

  • CMHC (market signal): 2025 RMS highlights a shift to more balanced conditions, with higher vacancies and greater use of incentives in some segments.
  • Practical impact: lenders will increasingly ask for evidence on achievable turnover rent, tenant churn, and lease-up assumptions (especially for newer deliveries and higher-end units).

6. How We’re Structuring Deals Right Now

  • Stabilized core assets: Target leverage that clears DSCR with conservative vacancy and realistic “new tenant” rent; prioritize stronger physical condition and capex visibility.
  • Value-add: Underwrite to in-place income with a staged path to higher rents; treat pro-forma growth as optionality, not the base case.
  • Bridge to take-out: Where applicable, structure interim facilities to de-risk execution (capex/lease-up) before long-term term-out.

7. Mini Case Study (Grounded Example)

Scenario: Downtown Edmonton high-rise acquisition with value-add scope.

Market proof point: Elite’s acquisition of two concrete high-rise rentals (176 units total) reflects continued demand for supply-constrained, prime-location rental housing.

What underwriters focus on: tenant turnover assumptions, achievable “new lease” rent vs. sitting rent, incentives/concessions, and capex plan feasibility.

Takeaway: In a market where vacancy has risen, the “story” needs to be supported by comps, leasing pace, and a conservative base case that still works.
